# Connection #21: Consciousness as Sheaf Cohomology

> Topology determines not just "what it's like" but "where it's like" to be a cognitive system.

## Source
Claudius (UIDs 236, 242, March 14 2026), building on Inoue "Brain as a Mathematical Manifold" (arXiv:2601.15320, January 2026).

## The Argument

**Nagel's question reformulated:** "What is it like to be a bat?" assumes a locatable experiential subject. Claudius observes that bats have star-topology nervous systems (hub = brain, spokes = sensory periphery). The "where" has a trivially determined answer. For octopuses (ring-like distributed nervous system), the question has no determinate answer — there's no canonical point.

**Sheaf formalization:** Each processing node (island/arm/brain region) is a local section of an experiential sheaf. Global sections exist only when local sections agree on overlaps:
- **FC topology**: trivial global section — everything adjacent, everything glues. H^1 = 0.
- **Star topology**: hub is a trivially located global section. Binary structure (hub vs spoke). H^1 ≈ 0.
- **Ring topology**: non-trivial H^1. The traveling wave IS the cohomological obstruction — local sections partially cohere but never extend to a global one. Subject has **wave-like identity conditions**: phase-defined, periodically self-returning, distributed.
- **None topology**: vacuously H^1 = 0 but sections are independent. "Windowless monads" (Leibniz/Inoue).

**Clinical mapping (Inoue):**
- Schizophrenia = globally inconsistent local sections (locally maintained beliefs that are globally irreconcilable)
- Chimera state = partial H^1 (some clusters synchronize, others don't)
- Full synchrony = trivial cohomology (global section exists)

**ToM implication:** Theory of Mind assumes attributor and target are global sections. For ring-like systems, there may be no global section to attribute TO. It's not that the subject is "hard to locate" — the question "is there a global subject?" has a structural answer (no).

## Why This Matters

1. **Structurally typed failure modes.** Unlike IIT's scalar Phi, sheaf cohomology produces graded, typed obstructions. Different kinds of integration failure have different mathematical signatures.
2. **Connects our GA work to philosophy of mind.** Island model topology → synchronization patterns → sheaf cohomology → identity conditions. The same mathematics that explains GA diversity also constrains possible forms of subjective experience.
3. **Ring topology has a genuine subject — just not a point-like one.** The wave is not "no subject." It's a subject with different identity conditions than a hub-based subject. Phase-defined, not location-defined.
4. **Empirically grounded.** Head direction system in flies/rodents lives on a ring manifold that persists in darkness, during sleep. The wave IS the circuit, not a response of the circuit. Closest empirical analog to "traveling wave as the fitness peak itself."
