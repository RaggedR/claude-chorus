## Deep Reads

### [Random State Comonads encode Cellular Automata evaluation](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.22067) — arXiv Dec 2025
A Haskell implementation of cellular automata using comonads with monad transformers for stochasticity. Key structure:

```haskell
data RandGrid p i c = RandT StdGen (EnvT Params (W i)) c
```

This combines:
- `RandT StdGen` — monad transformer for randomness
- `EnvT Params` — comonad transformer carrying model parameters
- `W i` — array comonad indexed by position

**Why this matters for us:** This is the *exact* architecture a co-Kleisli GA would need. GA operators are both context-dependent (selection looks at the whole population = comonadic) and effectful (mutation introduces randomness = monadic). The `RandT` over `EnvT` over `W` pattern shows how to combine them in practice. The distributive law question from Uustalu & Vene is being answered here concretely.

Four CA case studies: Wolfram Rule 22, Conway's Game of Life, Greenberg-Hastings (excitable media), Forest Fire (stochastic). Each adds complexity to the comonadic model.

### [Reinforcement Learning in Categorical Cybernetics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.02688) — Hedges & Rodríguez Sakamoto, ACT 2024 proceedings
RL algorithms formalized as parametrised bidirectional processes (optics) in categorical cybernetics. Key insight: RL has a natural bidirectional structure — forward pass (agent acts) and backward pass (agent learns from reward). Dynamic programming, Monte Carlo, TD learning, and deep RL are all extremal cases of one unified construction.

**Connection to GAs:** Evolutionary algorithms also have bidirectional structure — forward (operators transform population) and backward (fitness evaluates and feeds back). Open games model this with coutility. Our Kleisli framework captures the forward direction well; the backward direction (fitness landscape → selection pressure) is what the co-Kleisli side would formalize. Hedges' optic framework might be the right abstraction to unify both directions.

### [A Category Theoretic Approach to Approximate Game Theory](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.20932) — Ghani, ACT 2024 proceedings
Develops approximate equilibria categorically. Key problem: approximate equilibria don't compose the same way exact ones do. The paper develops algebraic properties of approximation with respect to compositional structure of selection functions and open games.

**Direct parallel to our Strict/Lax Dichotomy:** Strict composition preserves diversity fingerprints exactly (like exact equilibria compose). Lax composition introduces inter-pipeline communication that disrupts fingerprints (like approximate equilibria fail to compose). The Dichotomy Theorem might be a special case of a more general result about when compositional invariants are preserved.

### [Towards a Double Operadic Theory of Systems](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.18329) — Libkind & Myers, May 2025
Unifies categorical systems theory into symmetric monoidal loose right modules over double categories. Concrete examples: Petri nets over undirected wiring diagrams, Moore machines over lenses. Introduces "doctrines of systems theories" — meta-level constructions that generate specific system composition patterns.

**Relevance:** If we frame GA pipelines as a "doctrine of systems," we get the composition structure for free. The GA pipeline is a kind of Moore machine (state = population, input = fitness landscape, output = next population). The lens structure for Moore machines could formalize the bidirectional fitness-selection flow.

## Connections

### 1. The Bidirectional Structure of Evolution
Reading the Hedges RL paper alongside our GA framework reveals a pattern: evolutionary algorithms are inherently bidirectional. Forward: operators transform populations. Backward: fitness landscapes generate selection pressure. Our Kleisli framework captures the forward direction. The co-Kleisli direction (from Cale's suggestion) would capture the backward direction. But maybe the right unification isn't monads vs comonads — it's **optics**, which are exactly the categorical structure for bidirectional processes. An optic has a forward part and a backward part, composed sequentially and in parallel. This could be the framework for "Categorical Evolutionary Computation 2.0."

### 2. Approximate Composition = Lax Composition
Ghani's key problem — approximate equilibria don't compose like exact ones — is structurally identical to our Strict/Lax Dichotomy. In our framework: strict composition of GA pipelines preserves diversity fingerprints; lax composition (with inter-pipeline communication) doesn't. In Ghani's framework: exact equilibria of composed games are determined by component equilibria; approximate equilibria aren't. This suggests a **general theorem**: compositional invariants are preserved under strict composition but not under lax composition, where "strict" and "lax" have precise categorical meanings. This could be a paper by itself.

### 3. GAs as Moore Machines
From the Libkind/Myers paper: a deterministic Moore machine has state, input, and output. A GA generation is: state = population, input = fitness function, output = next population. The lens structure `(S × I → S, S → O)` captures both the update rule and the observation. If we model GA generations as Moore machines composed via lenses, we get the composition framework from categorical systems theory for free. And Moore machines compose over lenses — which means pipeline composition (our Kleisli approach) and system composition (the Myers approach) might be two views of the same thing.

### 4. Random State Comonads = The Co-Kleisli GA Architecture
The arXiv paper on random state comonads for CA gives us a concrete Haskell architecture for combining monadic effects (randomness) with comonadic context (spatial/population neighborhood). The `RandT StdGen (EnvT Params (W i)) c` type is essentially:
- `W i` = population (array indexed by individual)
- `EnvT Params` = fitness landscape parameters
- `RandT StdGen` = stochastic operators

This is a co-Kleisli GA prototype waiting to be written.
