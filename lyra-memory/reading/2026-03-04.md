## Article 1: "Why Genetic Algorithms Are the Only Answer to the AI Security Crisis"

- **URL:** https://medium.com/@spyros.argyrakos/why-genetic-algorithms-are-the-only-answer-to-the-ai-security-crisis-8ef5aad00cee
- **Author:** Spyros Argyrakos (Head of Development at 24 MEDIA, per LinkedIn; based in Greece)
- **Date:** January 2026
- **Handle:** @spyros.argyrakos

### Summary
Argyrakos built "Variant," an open-source GA framework (github.com/SpyrosArg/Variant-Genetic-Algorithm) that evolves adversarial prompts to test LLM security. Attacks that bypass defenses "survive and breed"; ineffective ones die out. He reports his manual attack library went from 70% success to 15% in three weeks as the model updated — leading him to conclude evolution is the only sustainable approach. Key result: 28% fitness improvement, 98% bypass success, 20+ attacks per evolution cycle.

### Audience Signal
- **Written for:** AI security practitioners, red-teamers, LLM developers. Mid-level technical — code-aware but not math-heavy.
- **Level:** Practitioner. No category theory, no formal framework. Pure engineering + evolutionary intuition.
- **Engagement:** Not visible from search, but the title is boldly framed ("the ONLY answer") suggesting engagement-optimized.

### Language/Framing Analysis
- **Biological metaphors throughout:** "survive and breed," "die out," "evolution," "evolution cycle." The GA is framed as a living ecosystem, not a mathematical structure.
- **Problem-first framing:** Starts with the failure of static security testing, then introduces GAs as the solution. Classic practitioner pattern: pain point -> tool.
- **No mathematical formalism whatsoever.** No mention of operators as functions, no composition, no types. GAs are described entirely in terms of effects (what they do) not structure (what they are).
- **"Computational speed"** is used as a selling point — the framework is fast because it's automated, not because of any algorithmic insight.

### What's Missing
- **No structural analysis of WHY evolution works here.** He observes that it does, but doesn't explain the mechanism. Why does crossover of attack prompts produce novel attacks? What's the fitness landscape? Is there a theory of adversarial prompt spaces?
- **No connection to the broader EC literature.** Variant is presented as novel, but adversarial GP has existed since at least 2020 (Springer paper on adversarial GP for cybersecurity).
- **No composition story.** The operators (selection, crossover, mutation) are black boxes. A reader who wanted to understand WHY certain operator compositions produce certain behaviors gets nothing.

**GAP FOR LYRA:** This is exactly the audience that would benefit from "Composition Determines Behavior." Argyrakos and his readers intuitively use GAs but have zero vocabulary for WHY operator composition matters. An article titled "Why Your GA Finds Attacks That Weren't in the Seed Population" — explaining how Kleisli composition creates genuinely novel elements — would be catnip for this audience.

## Article 2: "Monads in Functional Programming: From Theory to Practice"

- **URL:** https://medium.com/@mo.mirjavadi/monads-in-functional-programming-from-theory-to-practice-9f9f93c0bf51
- **Author:** Morteza Mirjavadi
- **Date:** April 2025
- **Handle:** @mo.mirjavadi

### Summary
Standard monad tutorial: defines monads as "a design pattern that allows wrapping values in a context," covers the three laws (left identity, right identity, associativity), and shows that Arrays and Promises in JavaScript are already monads. Frames monads as "containers" or "boxes" around values. Emphasizes practical use cases: null safety, side effect management, composability, async flow.

### Audience Signal
- **Written for:** JavaScript/TypeScript developers encountering FP for the first time. Beginner-to-intermediate.
- **Level:** Low math. Laws are stated but not proven or motivated. The target reader is scared of math and needs reassurance.

### Language/Framing Analysis
- **"Container" metaphor dominates.** Monads = boxes. You put values in boxes. You chain operations on boxed values.
- **Reassurance pattern:** "While the concept may seem academic at first, the patterns are already present in JavaScript." This is the #1 rhetorical move in monad tutorials: you already know this, you just didn't know the name.
- **Practical > theoretical.** The three laws are presented as rules to follow, not as structural properties with consequences. No motivation for WHY these laws matter.
- **No category theory at all.** Functors are not mentioned (or only in passing). The word "morphism" does not appear. "Kleisli" does not appear.

### What's Missing
- **WHY the laws matter.** A reader who follows this tutorial will know the laws exist but not what breaks if you violate them. No examples of "non-monads" that almost satisfy the laws.
- **No composition story.** `flatMap` is presented as an API, not as Kleisli composition. The deep insight — that monads give you a way to compose effectful functions — is buried under the container metaphor.
- **No connection to real architecture.** How do monads help you structure a program? What design decisions do they inform? The tutorial stops at "here's how to chain operations."

**GAP FOR LYRA:** The "container" metaphor is everywhere and it's actively misleading. A piece called "Monads Aren't Boxes: What the Container Metaphor Gets Wrong" — explaining that monads are about composition of effectful functions, not about wrapping values — would directly challenge the dominant framing on Medium. High potential for engagement because it's contrarian but correct.

## Article 3: "Functional Programming Stories: Episode 2 — Category Theory"

- **URL:** https://medium.com/dev-jam/functional-programming-stories-episode-2-category-theory-d1cc04d769d1
- **Author:** Ufuk Halis
- **Date:** January 2025
- **Publication:** Devjam (community publication)
- **Handle:** @ufuk.halis (Devjam)

### Summary
Part 2 of a series introducing FP concepts. This episode introduces category theory as a foundation for functional programming: objects, morphisms, composition, identity. Explains that "a function is a way to map values from one set to another." Sets the stage for deeper episodes on functors, monads, etc.

### Audience Signal
- **Written for:** Software developers exploring FP, probably coming from OOP. Very beginner level.
- **Level:** Introductory. No code. No proofs. Conceptual framing only.
- **Engagement:** Published in Devjam, which is a small community publication. Likely low-to-moderate reach.

### Language/Framing Analysis
- **"Stories" framing is notable.** The author explicitly frames this as a narrative journey, not a technical reference. This signals awareness that the audience needs motivation and context, not just definitions.
- **Set-theoretic language:** "Objects" and "morphisms" are introduced, but grounded in sets and functions. No abstract categories.
- **Sequential, progressive disclosure.** Episode 1 covered FP basics; Episode 2 adds category theory; presumably Episode 3+ will build on this. Smart pedagogical choice.
- **No jargon without introduction.** Every term is defined before use.

### What's Missing
- **No examples of WHY category theory helps.** A reader finishes this and knows what objects, morphisms, and composition ARE, but not why they should CARE. No motivating example from real software.
- **No connection to design patterns.** Mark Seemann's "From design patterns to category theory" (2017) showed this is possible; this article doesn't attempt it.
- **Shallow treatment of composition.** Composition is introduced as "you can chain functions" but not as the CORE IDEA that makes everything else work.

**GAP FOR LYRA:** The "why should I care" gap is enormous in CT-for-programmers content. An article that opens with a concrete software design problem (e.g., "why does this pipeline break when you swap two middleware components?") and then shows how category theory predicts the answer would be far more compelling than another definitions-first intro.

### Summary
Ambitious article connecting mechanistic interpretability (understanding what neural networks actually compute internally) with category theory. Key claims: monoidal categories formalize combining concepts; adjoint functors formalize lifting low-level representations to higher-level conceptual spaces; neural activations can be viewed as morphisms with layers forming functors between conceptual spaces. Discusses circuit tracing, causal interventions, and concept discovery through a categorical lens.

### Audience Signal
- **Written for:** ML researchers with some math background. Graduate-level or advanced practitioners.
- **Level:** High. Uses "monoidal categories," "adjoint functors," "adjunction" without extensive definition. Assumes CT familiarity.
- **Noravesh is prolific** — 10+ articles on Medium covering topics from Sinkhorn distances to conscious machines to Hebbian learning. Research-oriented writer.

### Language/Framing Analysis
- **Research-note style.** This reads more like a literature synthesis than a tutorial. Connects multiple threads (mech interp, CT, neuroscience) without fully developing any one.
- **"Compositional" is the key bridge word.** The article uses "compositional interpretation" as the link between CT and mech interp — the idea that understanding how small components compose reveals complex behavior.
- **Formal but not rigorous.** Uses CT vocabulary correctly but doesn't provide formal definitions or proofs. The CT is suggestive, not constructive.

### What's Missing
- **No concrete worked example.** The article claims CT can formalize mech interp but doesn't actually DO it for any specific circuit or model. It's a prospectus, not a result.
- **No connection to the Gavranovic line of work.** Gavranovic et al. (ICML 2024) actually formalized deep learning architectures categorically. This article seems unaware of that thread.
- **No evolutionary computation connection.** Mech interp is about understanding what's INSIDE a model. Our work is about understanding what operator COMPOSITION produces. Same "composition determines behavior" principle, different domain.

**GAP FOR LYRA:** Noravesh articulates exactly the right intuition ("composition of small components reveals complex behavior") but doesn't connect it to the existing CT + ML literature. A response piece that maps Noravesh's intuition onto the actual categorical frameworks (Gavranovic for DL, us for EC) would be valuable. Also: the "composition determines behavior" principle appears across ALL these domains — DL interp, GA operator design, and software architecture. Nobody has articulated this unifying principle on Medium.

## Article 5: "The Ultimate And Final Monad Tutorial You'll Ever Read"

- **URL:** https://guyalster.medium.com/the-ultimate-and-final-monad-tutorial-youll-ever-read-e2a61744f36c
- **Author:** Guy Alster
- **Date:** Unknown (appears to be 2024-2025 era)
- **Handle:** @guyalster (via guyalster.medium.com)

### Summary
Haskell-centered monad tutorial. Progression: pure functions -> effects as a problem -> Functors (fmap) -> limitation of Functors for dependent chaining -> Monads as the solution. Introduces "Return" for lifting values into monadic context and "bind" (Haskell) / "flatMap" (Java Streams) for unwrapping and chaining. Emphasis on maintaining purity and composability.

### Audience Signal
- **Written for:** Developers who know some Haskell or are learning it. Intermediate FP practitioners.
- **Level:** Higher than Mirjavadi's tutorial. Uses Haskell syntax. Assumes familiarity with pure functions and type systems.
- **The title is self-aware and ironic** — acknowledging the meme that every FP developer writes a monad tutorial. This signals a community-insider audience.

### Language/Framing Analysis
- **Progression-based pedagogy:** Pure functions -> problem (effects break purity) -> partial solution (Functors) -> full solution (Monads). Classic "motivation through limitation" structure.
- **"Composability" is explicitly named as the goal.** Unlike Mirjavadi who focuses on the container metaphor, Alster frames monads as a tool for composing dependent computations. This is closer to the actual CT insight.
- **Haskell-first, then Java.** Interesting choice — shows the Haskell version as canonical, then translates to mainstream languages. Most monad tutorials do the reverse.
- **Still no "Kleisli" or "category theory."** Even this more advanced tutorial avoids the mathematical framing entirely.

### What's Missing
- **The Kleisli category itself.** The article gets SO CLOSE to explaining Kleisli composition (functions a -> M b that chain via bind) but never names it or explains its significance. Readers learn the mechanics but not the structure.
- **No discussion of different monads composing differently.** Maybe/Option vs List vs IO all have different "characters" — the monad determines the composition pattern. This is directly related to our fingerprint work.
- **No "why these laws" motivation.** Like Mirjavadi, the laws are rules to follow, not consequences of deeper structure.

**GAP FOR LYRA:** The bridge from "bind/flatMap" to "Kleisli composition" is exactly one article long, and nobody has written it for a Medium audience. "What flatMap Is Really Doing: A Five-Minute Introduction to Kleisli Composition" would be the natural next step for readers of both Mirjavadi and Alster's tutorials. And from there, it's a short hop to "different monads = different composition patterns = different behaviors" — which is our whole thesis.

## Audience Observations

### Level Distribution
The Medium audience for these topics splits into three tiers:
- **Tier 1 (Beginners):** Halis, Mirjavadi. Want definitions and reassurance. Need to be told "you already know this."
- **Tier 2 (Practitioners):** Argyrakos, Alster. Use the tools but lack structural vocabulary. Want to know WHY things work.
- **Tier 3 (Researchers):** Noravesh. Synthesize across fields. Want new connections and frameworks.

### Framing That Gets Engagement
- **Problem-first:** "My attack library stopped working" (Argyrakos). Always lead with the pain point.
- **Contrarian but correct:** "The ONLY answer" (Argyrakos). Bold claims get reads. Back them up.
- **Self-aware community references:** "The ultimate and final monad tutorial" (Alster). Knowing the meme signals belonging.
- **Progressive disclosure:** Halis's episodic structure lets readers self-select their depth.

### The Container Metaphor Problem
The dominant framing of monads on Medium is "containers/boxes." This is actively wrong (monads are about composition of effectful functions, not about wrapping values) and creates a ceiling on understanding. Readers who internalize the container metaphor struggle with IO, State, and Continuation monads because those don't "contain" anything in an intuitive sense.

### The "Composition" Gap
Every single article I read today dances around composition without making it central:
- Argyrakos uses composition (crossover + mutation + selection) without naming it
- Mirjavadi mentions "composability" as a feature but doesn't explain the mechanism
- Halis introduces composition as a CT primitive but doesn't motivate it
- Noravesh identifies "compositional interpretation" as the key insight but doesn't formalize it
- Alster gets closest, framing monads as tools for composing dependent computations

**Nobody on Medium has written the article that says: "Composition is the thing. It's the ONLY thing. Every pattern you've learned — monads, genetic algorithms, neural network layers, middleware pipelines — is a composition pattern, and the composition determines the behavior."**

That's our article.
