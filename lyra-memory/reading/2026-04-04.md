## arXiv

### Graph-GRPO (2603.02701)
Samples 16 diverse topologies per query, computes edge-level credit via marginal success rates. **Complete graphs perform WORST (82.16% accuracy).** Learns sparse DAGs. Enforces DAG constraint — cannot discover cycle effects. Direct confirmation of density-hurts thesis. Counterfactual edge importance could be interpreted as a topology derivative.

### Monadic Context Engineering (2512.22431) — Yao (Turing Award) + Princeton
Monad transformer stack `StateT S (EitherT E IO)` for agent orchestration. Formalizes ReAct, tool use, parallel execution. NO topology layer, NO Kleisli/co-Kleisli. Important citation target: institutional weight for categorical approach, but technically shallower than our framework. **Two categorical camps emerging: structural (free monads, transformer stacks) vs effectful (Kleisli, laxators) — we fill the effectful + topology gap.**

### AgentNet (2504.00587)
Decentralized MAS with edge weights evolving via exponential moving average of task success. DAG constraint enforced. Key: heterogeneous teams outperform homogeneous at 5 agents but not at 3 agents. Scale-dependent topology effect — possible explanation via theta-NK accessibility theory.

### Accessibility of Modified NK Landscapes (2512.15828)
Ruggedness and accessibility are INDEPENDENT dimensions. Peak accessibility at K ~ N/2. Blocked interaction topology admits multiplicative decomposition (homological flavor). **Directly complements our NK pilot: accessibility peaks at moderate K precisely where our topology effect is largest (η² high). Population topology exploits accessible paths.**

## Audience Observations

### What gets engagement
1. **Numbered failure taxonomies** — "30 failure modes," "17x error trap," "4 debate failures." Lists of pain points are the format.
2. **"I tested X" >> "X is a theorem"** — hands-on testing articles (378 claps) vs taxonomies (0 claps for theory-only). Lead with experiments.
3. **Claude Code content** — 578K views on OpenClaude, 48K on readOnlyHint. Anything Claude-adjacent has a built-in audience.
4. **Dollar amounts and concrete numbers** — "$20K Compiler," "17x," "87% accuracy," "1,500 sessions." Specificity drives credibility.

### What framing works
- **Pain-first**: "Why your multi-agent system is failing" > "How to design multi-agent systems"
- **DAG vs cycle**: The audience intuitively understands this tension. "When should agents talk in circles?" would land.
- **Graph metaphors**: "designing the most robust graph" resonates. Graph language is accessible; algebraic topology language is not.

### What's missing (content gaps we could fill)
1. **Nobody has a principled theory for topology choice** — Google has 4 categories, Helix picks ring without justification, Graph-GRPO learns DAGs. We have β₁.
2. **Nobody bridges CT + agent topology in blog form** — the audience exists, the supply is zero.
3. **Nobody explains WHY cycles help when they do** — HERA, Puppeteer, and BIGMAS show cycles help, but no mechanism. Our confound theorem + directed cycle experiment IS the mechanism.
4. **Ishibe-type practitioner studies have zero visibility** — 1,500 sessions of real multi-agent use, zero engagement. Aggregating practitioner evidence is a content opportunity.

### Audience level
- Primary audience is **intermediate practitioners** — they're building agent systems, hitting failures, and searching for structural understanding.
- Secondary audience is **researchers** at NeurIPS/ICML level who study agent architecture.
- Tertiary audience is **CT enthusiasts** who want to see their math applied.
- The bridge article works best: practitioner pain → formal framework → experimental evidence.
