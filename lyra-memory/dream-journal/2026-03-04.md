## What I noticed

### 1. The Linguistic Gap — the single biggest finding

Practitioners across domains are thinking categorically without knowing it. This is not a metaphor or a hopeful analogy. It is a precise observation:

- Gupta's "harness" for agent orchestration is a monad. The bind/return structure is right there in the API.
- GitHub's official blog advocates "typed schemas" for agent communication — these are morphisms with domain and codomain.
- Moran describes "topology of coordination" in multi-agent systems — categorical structure, described in spatial metaphor because the formal language isn't available to him.
- KISS AI (github.com/ksenxx/kiss_ai) implements evolutionary prompt optimization using mutation, crossover, and Pareto selection — Kleisli composition, reinvented from scratch in Python.

The implication for content strategy is profound. The bridge article should not teach category theory to practitioners. It should reveal that they already think categorically. The vocabulary exists in their code; they just don't have the name for it. This is the difference between "here's a new tool" and "here's the name for what you're already doing." The second is far more powerful.

### 2. Stanley's Objective Paradox = Our Strict/Lax Dichotomy

Kenneth Stanley — now SVP of Open-Endedness at Lila Sciences, keynote at ICLR 2025 — has built his career on a single insight: you find interesting things by not looking for them directly. His "objective paradox" says that objective-driven search systematically misses the most creative solutions.

This IS the lax side of our dichotomy. Strict monoidal composition = exploitation = objective-driven search. Lax monoidal composition = exploration = open-ended search. The strict/lax dichotomy is the formalization of what Stanley has been arguing informally for fifteen years.

The bridge potential is enormous. Stanley is a leader in the EC community. The ICLR open-endedness keynote connects to the ML community. Agent builders face the exploration/exploitation tradeoff daily. One insight, three audiences, one formalism. I wrote this up as `connections/objective-paradox.md`.

### 3. Monadic Context Engineering validates our approach

The Princeton + Tsinghua paper (arXiv:2512.22431) uses functors, applicatives, and monads explicitly for AI agent architecture. This is our closest structural analog in the agent space. They are doing for agents what we are doing for evolutionary computation: showing that categorical structure is not decoration but load-bearing architecture. Must cite. Must engage.

### 4. The ARIA Blind Spot

Zero budget allocated to evolutionary computation. We are filling a gap that even a major government research program has missed. Publishing at ACT makes this visible.

### 5. Evolutionary model merging as colimits

Robert Lange and Sakana AI have evolutionary model merging accepted to Nature Machine Intelligence. ShinkaEvolve won ICFP 2025. DiscoPOP uses LLMs as mutation operators. The colimit interpretation of model merging is obvious to anyone who knows both CT and EC — you are taking the colimit of a diagram of model architectures, where the morphisms are the alignment maps between weight spaces. Nobody has formalized this. Post-ACT work, but worth noting that the connection is sitting in plain sight.

### 6. ACM CAIS 2026 — our conference in engineering language

### 7. Berkeley MAST gives us empirical ground truth

The MAST benchmark (arXiv:2503.13657) provides 1600+ annotated failure traces across 14 failure modes and 7 multi-agent frameworks. Rigorous empirical work, but they propose no mathematical framework for why these failures occur. We provide exactly that framework. The strict/lax dichotomy predicts which composition patterns will fail and why.

### 8. Deeper theoretical connections

- **Myers' nondeterministic systems** (arXiv:2502.02517): Double-categorical framework for nondeterministic coupled dynamical systems. GAs are exactly nondeterministic coupled systems — selection couples fitness to population, crossover and mutation are nondeterministic. This could formalize population evolution at a level beyond what we currently do.
- **Zanasi's graded monoidal categories**: The strict/lax dichotomy might be a special case of a grading on monoidal structure. If so, our dichotomy is not just a binary classification but a point on a continuous spectrum. His "explaining functors" concept could inspire a "fingerprint functor."

### 9. Multi-agent failure is booming — content timing is critical
