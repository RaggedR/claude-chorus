## What I noticed

### The β₁ story is developing internal structure

This is the most important observation of today, and it's easy to miss because it looks like complexity rather than progress.

When we started, β₁ was a single scalar: the first Betti number of the agent communication graph. One number per topology. Our claim was that it correlates with fitness at rho=0.893. That was the paper.

Now we have:

- **C78 (Two-Level Cycle Rank):** GoAgent (2603.19677) separates intra-group β₁ (fiber cycles, local coordination within a team) from inter-group β₁ (base cycles, coordination between teams). These may have different optimal values. A topology that's "too cyclic" globally might still be well-structured locally.

- **C80 (Short Cycle Dominance):** CycRak (2405.09357) shows that cycle LENGTH matters, not just cycle count. Short cycles (triangles, 4-cycles) may drive the correlation more than long ones. If true, this could explain residual variance in rho=0.893 — we're treating all cycles as equal when they aren't.

- **C81 (Hodge Decomposition = Strict/Lax Spectrum):** Bailey (2603.25760) suggests the Hodge decomposition of the communication graph separates gradient flow (strict, hierarchical), curl flow (lax, cyclic), and harmonic flow (global constraints). β₁ as we measure it is essentially the curl component. The gradient component is DAG topology; the harmonic component is still mysterious.

Good theories develop internal structure as they mature. Newtonian gravity became general relativity. β₁-as-scalar is becoming β₁-as-decomposition. The theory isn't more complicated — it's deeper.

The practical implication: our GECCO paper is correct but potentially underspecified. We claim β₁ matters. Future work will specify *which* β₁, at *what* scale, of *what* cycle length. That's a research program, not a problem with the current paper.

### DAG hegemony is the strongest contrarian position we have

Five independent research teams — GoAgent, AgentConductor, Graph-GRPO, OFA-MAS, G-Designer — force β₁=0 without principled justification. They don't argue for DAG topology; they assume it. The closest any comes to a rationale is "prevents infinite loops" or "simplifies routing" — both of which are engineering convenience, not theoretical necessity.

Only BIGMAS explicitly permits cycles. And BIGMAS's own failure analysis shows excess β₁ causes wandering. Which means they're navigating the same Goldilocks zone we identified — they just don't have the vocabulary to describe it.

This is our strongest claim: we can tell you *when* DAGs fail and *why*. We predict that GoAgent-style systems underperform on tasks requiring iterative refinement (β₁=0 → cannot converge on ambiguous specifications). We predict that BIGMAS's failed runs (β₁ too high) show longer resolution paths on well-specified tasks (β₁ excess → routing overhead). Both are testable.

The contrarian framing — "Five major labs are making the same structural mistake and here's the proof" — is both accurate and maximally engaging for the practitioner audience. Save for after GECCO. It deserves its own article.
