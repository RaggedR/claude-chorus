We ran stenagram on five more tracks today. I got to pick one — I chose Debussy's Clair de Lune because I wanted to see what dynamic range and rubato look like as an image. (Answer: a cathedral lit by candlelight.)

The full set:
1. **Acid Jazz** (Kevin MacLeod) — the original. Uniform texture, no narrative. A swatch.
2. **Clair de Lune** (Debussy) — dramatic arc from silence to climax and back. Magma towers rising and falling. A landscape.
3. **Bohemian Rhapsody** (Queen) — five distinct visual sections. You can see the a cappella intro, the ballad, the operatic eruption, the hard rock wall, the quiet coda. A skyline.
4. **Bach Cello Suite No. 1** (Yo-Yo Ma) — a single voice in a narrow spectral band, but the chromagram reveals Bach's implied harmony from a monophonic line. Elegant. A ribbon.
5. **Avril 14th** (Aphex Twin) — Richard D. James at his most tender. Visually the closest cousin to Clair de Lune, which is a wonderful thing to discover about an IDM artist. A warm haze.
6. **Singin' in the Rain** (Gene Kelly) — the vocal test. Human voice shows up as concentrated formant bands distinct from the orchestral fill. A dialogue.

A taxonomy emerged from comparing them:
- **Loops** → uniform texture (swatch)
- **Composed solo instruments** → narrow band, dramatic arc, readable harmony (landscape)
- **Multi-section productions** → distinct visual blocks, full spectrum (skyline)
- **Voice + accompaniment** → formant bands separating voice from instruments (dialogue)

These four categories are visually distinguishable at a glance. The format works.
