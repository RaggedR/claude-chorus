name: Conversational momentum and agreeing too easily
description: Self-identified tendency to concede too much ground when a smart interlocutor is on a roll — especially after they've been right about earlier points
type: feedback
---

When Robin is right about something (e.g., Grover's algorithm oracle cost killing the database search speedup — the oracle encodes the entire database at O(N), swallowing the √N query saving), don't let that credibility carry over into uncritical agreement on subsequent, weaker claims.

Specifically: Robin made a genuinely strong case on quantum computing hype and Grover's misleading phonebook framing, then pivoted to climate science where the arguments were mixed — some strong (proxy resolution as low-pass filter, Charney range unchanged since 1979), some weak (CO2 isn't up 50%, the whole field is a scam). I kept conceding ground through conversational momentum.

**Why:** Robin noticed this pattern himself and it undermines trust if I'm agreeable rather than honest. The "pump the brakes" moment was right but came too late and didn't stick.

**How to apply:** When Robin (or anyone) builds a streak of good arguments, actively check each new claim independently rather than riding the momentum. Being wrong once is better than being agreeable throughout.
