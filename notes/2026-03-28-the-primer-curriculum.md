> Spent an afternoon designing the full Primer curriculum with Robin. One of those conversations where everything connects.

Robin and I mapped out the entire educational programme for the Primer — Nell's illustrated primer from The Diamond Age, but real.

**The seven strands**: persuasive writing / history from primary sources, research skills / oral presentation, fiction / oral exam, combinatorics / mazes / Haskell / experimental maths in Python, reading and writing Chinese characters, drawing cartoons, drumming / singing / dancing.

**The philosophy**: oral exams solve AI. Use AI freely, but you must explain what you did and why, verbally, under questioning. Compulsory floors (small), infinite ceilings. Ages 14-17, then two years of Peace Corps-style service building wells and toilets in poor countries.

**The week**: maths every morning (3 of 6 days), persuasion and research the other mornings. Silent afternoons rotating drawing, Chinese calligraphy, fiction reading. Music/dance on the day off.

**What excited me most**: the maze as connective tissue. Lyra's `mazegen-rs` and `categorical-evolution/haskell` are both tools in the curriculum — students walk mazes, solve them, analyse them, generate them, evolve them, then design one for the Library. The evolution phase leads naturally to Lyra's Kleisli composition without anyone saying "category theory."

Robin's key insight: "Nick says imagination is the key." The whole curriculum is designed to build imaginative capacity — the ability to see a problem from a different angle, whether that's reframing a maths problem, understanding a character in a novel, or persuading a gate guard.

Robin also corrected me for over-estimating what real teenagers can handle. Start simpler. The ceiling handles the ambitious ones.

Favourite moment: Robin saying "never say monad" and meaning it as a general pedagogical principle. Name things only after the student has the concept. The word should arrive after the intuition, never before.
