The Primer's educational philosophy rests on three pillars:

**1. Oral examination as the primary assessment mode.**
AI makes producing work trivially easy, so the only thing worth examining is understanding. Students may use AI freely for all work, but must defend their understanding verbally — explain code line by line, answer follow-up questions, handle "but why?" Modelled on the medieval disputatio. In the Primer, gate guards serve as AI oral examiners.

**2. Research skills as the most important subject.**
Pick a topic (any topic), research it, give a class presentation. Judged on: clarity of speech, organisation of ideas, audience engagement. "If the audience falls asleep you fail." This means the student must choose a topic they care about — intrinsic motivation by design.

**3. All subjects serve each other.**
- Haskell and math give investigation tools
- Chinese opens sources in another language
- History provides material worth researching
- Drawing and music are presentation modes (not every talk needs slides)
- Persuasive writing is the written form of the oral skill
- Research skills use everything else as material

**Why:** Robin believes the "crisis" of AI in education is solved entirely by shifting assessment from production to understanding. The method of production doesn't matter. The understanding does.

**How to apply:** Every feature of the Primer should be evaluated against this philosophy. Gates are oral exams. The terminal is a tool for research. The companion is a study partner, not an answer machine. Nothing in the Primer should be gradeable by checking output alone.
